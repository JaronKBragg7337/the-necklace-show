# The Necklace

*The Necklace* is an interactive Three.js story about Jalen Creed, a sailor who discovers the Weaver: a necklace that can reveal the memories held by objects, places, and the world around them.

This repository contains a fifteen-chapter cinematic web experience built with Three.js. It is being developed in public so people can experience the story, learn from the implementation, improve it, and imagine entirely different uses for its central power.

## The superpower at the heart of it

What if touching something allowed you to witness anything it had ever witnessed?

A tire mark could show the vehicle that made it, when it happened, and why. An ancient tree could reveal the day it was planted and every version of the world that grew around it. A medieval sword could remember the king who held it. Bones at an archaeological site could reveal a life that history forgot.

The power is not simply “seeing the past.” It is living with the weight of truth: knowing what happened does not automatically give you evidence, permission, justice, or the ability to convince anyone else.

## Why this is open

I first imagined this power in fifth grade.

My class was asked to choose a superpower and write about how it would improve our lives. I listened to everyone else's ideas first. There was teleportation for getting inside a bank, invisibility for sneaking into places, flight for traveling quickly, and many variations of the familiar powers from mainstream superheroes. I liked the advantages people imagined, but I felt that we were barely touching what a superpower could be.

I had always loved history and observed how other people thought, so I went home trying to find a different answer. Nothing came. I played *Age of Empires*, gave up for the night, and went to bed.

On the way to school the next morning, I noticed tire marks in the road. I wished I could see the moment they appeared—which vehicle made them, how they got there, and why. Then I passed what looked like the oldest tree in town. I wished I could see the world on the day it was planted and compare it with everything the tree had witnessed since.

That was the moment the idea arrived: **what if anything I touched could show me anything it had ever seen?**

Since then, whenever I have asked someone which superpower they would choose, I have shared this one too. I have always told people they are free to dream with it. A superpower should be a creative starting point, not a fence around one person's imagination.

*The Necklace* is one story built from that starting point. It is not meant to be the only story, the definitive version, or a claim that nobody else may explore the power. Any kid should be able to imagine it. Any creator should be able to ask what it would mean in their own world.

## Where else could the idea go?

The same power could lead to very different stories:

- An FBI agent touches something at a crime scene and learns the truth, but cannot reveal how they know it. They still have to obtain lawful evidence while watching a guilty person walk free.
- An archaeologist touches bones, pottery, a buried city, or a medieval weapon and encounters history from the perspective of the objects that survived it.
- A detective discovers that knowing the truth and proving the truth are different problems.
- A historian finds that official records conflict with what places and artifacts remember.
- A family encounters memories preserved in a home, heirloom, photograph, or tree.
- A horror story asks what happens when an object remembers something no human mind was meant to carry.
- A game turns every environment into an archive, with investigation shaped by which objects the player chooses to touch and which memories they can endure.
- A film, series, comic, or novel changes the rules, costs, limits, culture, characters, and consequences completely.

Those are examples, not boundaries. The possibilities are the point.

## An invitation, not a claim

Use the premise. Transform it. Give it different rules. Put it in another genre, time, culture, medium, or moral dilemma. Create something I would never have thought to create.

If you build from it, please keep the same door open for the next person. Do not treat the underlying superpower as creative territory that only you or your company should be allowed to explore. Your characters, world, writing, art, and particular expression can be yours; the shared imaginative seed should remain something people can dream with.

## Another way movies can be made

Everything in the original version of this experience was created from a single prompt to Kimi AI K3.

The complete source book, untouched generated ZIP, checksum, and extracted original are preserved in the [canonical archive](./canonical-archive/README.md). The project in the repository root is the continuing production version and includes later human- and tool-assisted improvements.

That fact is not the finish line or an excuse for rough edges. It is the starting point of a larger experiment: showing that a person can begin with a story and direct it into a moving, explorable world without first having access to a traditional film studio.

This is what excites me about the project beyond the story itself. It points toward another form of filmmaking—one in which a single prompt can produce the first complete prototype, and the creator can then use animation, 3D modeling, editing, sound, performance, and visual-effects tools to shape that prototype into something intentional and beautiful.

The goal of improving *The Necklace* is not merely to show that AI can generate a movie-like experience. The goal is to discover how good this new form can look when generation is followed by taste, direction, revision, and craft. The applications used to refine it may change over time; the important part is the workflow they make possible:

**story → generated first production → human direction → finished cinematic experience**

This repository keeps that process visible so other people can study it, improve it, and try their own versions. The exciting question is no longer only, “Can one person make something like a movie this way?” It is, “How far can one person take it?”

## Current experience

- Fifteen cinematic chapters
- Procedural 3D environments and recurring character designs
- Chapter navigation, captions, ambient sound, and optional recorded narration support
- Distinct visual languages for memories, Seeker technology, and the Heart of the Tides
- No account or external service required

## Run locally

Serve this directory with any static web server and open `index.html` through that server. Opening the file directly may prevent browser modules and narration assets from loading correctly.

## Reuse and licensing

The intent is for this project to be genuinely reusable, not merely visible. Before the public repository is released, it needs an explicit license covering the code and included assets. Exact story text, recordings, models, and third-party assets may need separate terms.

Until those license files are added, the repository's public availability alone does not grant reuse rights. This note is here to keep the legal status honest while the final licensing structure is chosen.
